## General danger signs
<!-- page: 5 | type: danger-signs -->
Check every sick child age 2 months up to 5 years for general danger signs.
ASK: Is the child able to drink or breastfeed? Does the child vomit everything? Has the child had convulsions?
LOOK: See if the child is lethargic or unconscious. Is the child convulsing now?
A child with ANY general danger sign needs URGENT attention; complete the assessment and any pre-referral treatment immediately so referral is not delayed.
If a general danger sign is present, classify as VERY SEVERE DISEASE (pink): give diazepam if convulsing now, give the first dose of an appropriate antibiotic, treat to prevent low blood sugar, keep the child warm, and refer URGENTLY.

## Cough or difficult breathing — assess
<!-- page: 6 | type: protocol -->
Does the child have cough or difficult breathing? If yes, ASK: For how long?
LOOK, LISTEN, FEEL (child must be calm): Count the breaths in one minute. Look for chest indrawing. Look and listen for stridor. Look and listen for wheezing.
Fast breathing thresholds: a child 2 months up to 12 months has fast breathing at 50 breaths per minute or more; a child 12 months up to 5 years has fast breathing at 40 breaths per minute or more.
If a pulse oximeter is available, determine oxygen saturation and refer if below 90%.

## Cough or difficult breathing — classify and treat
<!-- page: 6 | type: treatment -->
SEVERE PNEUMONIA OR VERY SEVERE DISEASE (pink): any general danger sign, OR stridor in a calm child. Give the first dose of an appropriate antibiotic and refer URGENTLY to hospital.
PNEUMONIA (yellow): chest indrawing OR fast breathing. Give oral amoxicillin for 5 days. If wheezing, give an inhaled bronchodilator for 5 days. Soothe the throat and relieve the cough with a safe remedy. Advise the mother when to return immediately. Follow up in 3 days. If coughing for more than 14 days or recurrent wheeze, refer for possible TB or asthma assessment.
COUGH OR COLD (green): no signs of pneumonia or very severe disease. Soothe the throat and relieve the cough with a safe remedy. Advise the mother when to return immediately. Follow up in 5 days if not improving.

## Diarrhoea — assess and classify dehydration
<!-- page: 7 | type: protocol -->
Does the child have diarrhoea? If yes, ASK: For how long? Is there blood in the stool?
LOOK and FEEL: the child's general condition (lethargic or unconscious; restless and irritable). Look for sunken eyes. Offer the child fluid: is the child not able to drink or drinking poorly, or drinking eagerly/thirsty? Pinch the skin of the abdomen: does it go back very slowly (longer than 2 seconds) or slowly?
SEVERE DEHYDRATION (pink): two of the following — lethargic or unconscious; sunken eyes; not able to drink or drinking poorly; skin pinch goes back very slowly.
SOME DEHYDRATION (yellow): two of the following — restless/irritable; sunken eyes; drinks eagerly/thirsty; skin pinch goes back slowly.
NO DEHYDRATION (green): not enough signs to classify as some or severe dehydration.

## Diarrhoea — treatment plans
<!-- page: 7 | type: treatment -->
Plan A (no dehydration): treat at home. Give extra fluid, continue feeding, and advise when to return. Give ORS solution after each loose stool. Give zinc supplements daily for 10–14 days.
Plan B (some dehydration): give ORS solution in the clinic over 4 hours. Reassess and classify dehydration. Continue feeding. Give zinc supplements.
Plan C (severe dehydration): give IV fluids urgently. If IV is not possible, give ORS by nasogastric tube. Refer urgently for IV treatment if not available.

## Fever — assess and classify
<!-- page: 8 | type: protocol -->
Does the child have fever (by history, feels hot, or temperature 37.5°C or above)? Decide the malaria risk and ASK how long the fever has been present.
LOOK and FEEL: for a stiff neck, runny nose, and signs of MEASLES (generalized rash and cough/runny nose/red eyes).
VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE (pink): any general danger sign OR a stiff neck. Give the first dose of an appropriate antibiotic, treat to prevent low blood sugar, give one dose of paracetamol for high fever, and refer URGENTLY.
MALARIA (yellow, in malaria-risk areas, positive test): give a recommended first-line oral antimalarial. Give one dose of paracetamol for high fever. Advise when to return immediately. Follow up in 3 days if fever persists.

## Ear problem — assess and classify
<!-- page: 9 | type: protocol -->
Does the child have an ear problem? ASK: Is there ear pain? Is there ear discharge, and for how long? LOOK and FEEL for tender swelling behind the ear and for ear discharge.
MASTOIDITIS (pink): tender swelling behind the ear. Give the first dose of an appropriate antibiotic, give the first dose of paracetamol for pain, and refer URGENTLY.
ACUTE EAR INFECTION (yellow): ear pain, OR discharge for less than 14 days. Give an oral antibiotic for 5 days, give paracetamol for pain, and dry the ear by wicking. Follow up in 5 days.

## Acute malnutrition — assess and classify
<!-- page: 10 | type: protocol -->
Check for acute malnutrition. LOOK and FEEL: for oedema of both feet. Determine weight-for-height/length (WFH/L) and measure mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) in a child 6 months or older.
SEVERE ACUTE MALNUTRITION (pink): oedema of both feet, OR WFH/L less than -3 z-scores, OR MUAC less than 115 mm. Assess for complications and appetite; if complicated, refer URGENTLY.
MODERATE ACUTE MALNUTRITION (yellow): WFH/L between -3 and -2 z-scores, OR MUAC 115 up to 125 mm. Assess the child's feeding and counsel the mother. Follow up in 7 days.

## Amoxicillin dosing (oral)
<!-- page: 6 | type: dosing -->
Oral amoxicillin is the first-line antibiotic for PNEUMONIA (give twice daily for 5 days). Approximate dosing by weight using 250 mg tablets or syrup: 4 up to 10 kg, give 250 mg per dose; 10 up to 14 kg, give 375 mg; 14 up to 19 kg, give 500 mg. Always check the national formulary for the exact age/weight band before dosing.
